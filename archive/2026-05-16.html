🧬 Mito Research
  • Based on articles retrieved from PubMed: A peer-reviewed case report published May 1 in Medicine (Baltimore) describes identical twin brothers, both confirmed with the m.3243A>G mutation, who developed MELAS with strikingly different presentations — the elder with seizures and exercise intolerance from age 10, the younger with hearing loss at 12 followed by epilepsy. EEG showed widespread mixed high-amplitude slow waves; MRI revealed the characteristic migratory lesions in both. Their mother was an asymptomatic carrier at ~30% heteroplasmy. The paper documents the treatment regimen used (levetiracetam, L-arginine, coenzyme Q10, levocarnitine, ATP disodium) and emphasizes that MELAS's wide phenotypic variability — even between genetically identical individuals — frequently causes delayed or missed diagnosis; early genetic testing was key. (Medicine — Wu et al. — DOI 10.1097/MD.0000000000047264 — PubMed 42065208)
  • Based on articles retrieved from PubMed: A study published May 13 in Scientific Reports (Nature portfolio) found that mitochondrial transplantation — delivering healthy donor mitochondria (PN-101, derived from umbilical cord mesenchymal stem cells) into patient cells — significantly shifted heteroplasmy toward wild-type mtDNA in human cells carrying the m.1555A>G hearing-loss mutation, alongside measurable increases in ATP output and complex I (OXPHOS) activity. Repeated treatments sustained and enhanced the shift. This research involves a different mtDNA mutation than MELAS, but the core mechanism — external mitochondria altering the mutant-to-wild-type ratio in patient cells — is directly relevant to conditions driven by high mutant load such as m.3243A>G. ⚠️ Industry-funded: lead researchers are affiliated with Paean Biotechnology, Inc., maker of PN-101. (Scientific Reports — Kim et al. — DOI 10.1038/s41598-026-51402-4 — PubMed 42115265)
