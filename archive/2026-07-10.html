🧬 Mito Research
  • A Juntendo University/Chiba Children's Hospital study of 164 mitochondrial disease patients found that urine tests detect substantially more mutant mtDNA than blood tests for m.3243A>G — Emi's exact mutation — with urine heteroplasmy averaging 76.6% versus just 20.9% in paired blood samples from the same patients (n=80), and in 1 of 4 patients, blood testing alone would have missed a clinically meaningful heteroplasmy level (≥10%) that urine testing caught. This doesn't change treatment, but it's a concrete, low-burden diagnostic improvement — non-invasive urine sampling gives a truer picture of mutant load than blood draws, which matters for monitoring and future eligibility for heteroplasmy-targeting trials. (Molecular Genetics and Metabolism — PubMed)
