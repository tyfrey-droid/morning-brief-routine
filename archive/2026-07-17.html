🌍 World
  • Iran strikes Gulf states — On a sixth straight night, U.S. forces widened their strikes inside Iran to civilian-adjacent infrastructure — six bridges in Hormozgan province and a tower at the Chabahar port — in President Trump's push to force Tehran to reopen the Strait of Hormuz. Iran answered by firing missiles and drones at U.S.-allied Gulf states, striking a combined power-and-desalination plant in Kuwait and targeting sites in Qatar, Bahrain and Jordan; Qatar, a mediator in the conflict, condemned the attacks as neither side signaled any move to de-escalate. (Al Jazeera, PBS NewsHour)
  • France passes right-to-die law — France's National Assembly gave final approval, 291–241, to a landmark bill allowing adults with incurable, painful illnesses to obtain a lethal substance to self-administer — or to have a doctor or nurse administer it if they physically cannot. President Macron praised the "respectful debate," and Prime Minister Sébastien Lecornu will refer the law to the Constitutional Council before it takes effect, capping years of debate and repeated resistance from the Senate. (France 24, CNN)
🇺🇸 US & Politics
  • Trump revives election-fraud claims — In a Thursday-night primetime address, President Trump repeated debunked claims about past U.S. elections, alleging Chinese "meddling" in 2020 and announcing the release of previously classified documents on the 2020 and 2018 cycles; fact-checkers said the material showed no evidence any votes were altered. Democrats including Sen. Mark Warner accused the president of trying to pre-emptively delegitimize the 2026 midterms, noting intelligence agencies found China did not change a single 2020 vote. ABC, NBC and CNN declined to carry the speech live. (PBS NewsHour, CNBC)
  • Half of House Democrats break on Israel aid — A Republican-authored amendment from Rep. Thomas Massie to strip $3.3 billion in U.S. military aid to Israel failed 104–314, but drew 104 Democratic votes — roughly half the caucus and the largest congressional break yet from decades of bipartisan support. Party leaders split, with Minority Leader Hakeem Jeffries voting no and Whip Katherine Clark voting yes, in what reporting called a generational shift driven by the Gaza war and changing public opinion ahead of the midterms. (CBS News, NBC News, The Hill)
