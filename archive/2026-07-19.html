🇺🇸 US & Politics
  • Endangered species lose automatic shield — The Interior Department scrapped the 50-year-old "blanket 4(d) rule" that automatically extended most endangered-species protections — including the federal ban on "take" — to newly listed threatened species. Going forward, each of the roughly 30 species proposed for listing, from monarch butterflies to alligator snapping turtles and California spotted owls, will need an individualized plan, a slower process critics warn opens the door to drilling, mining and development. (PBS NewsHour, The Philadelphia Inquirer)
  • Green cards tied to public-benefit use — The Department of Homeland Security revived and expanded the "public charge" rule, directing officers to weigh applicants' use of Medicaid, food stamps and housing assistance when deciding green-card applications — benefits the Biden-era version had excluded. DHS estimates the change could affect up to 588,000 people applying from inside the U.S. each year; it publishes in the Federal Register on July 20 and takes effect September 18. (The Boston Globe, The Hill)

✨ One Good Thing
  • Why some toads outlast a killer fungus — Studying midwife toads in the Pyrenees, researchers worked out why some amphibian populations recover from chytrid fungus, a leading driver of global frog and toad declines: survivors develop mature immune defenses while still tadpoles, giving them a head start before the fungus strikes after metamorphosis. The finding offers a concrete roadmap for protecting threatened species. (ScienceDaily)
