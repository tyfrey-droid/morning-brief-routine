🌍 World
  • US disables a tanker and strikes new Iranian targets as Tehran threatens the Red Sea. In the sharpest escalation since the June truce frayed, U.S. forces disabled the tanker M/T Belma with Hellfire missiles as it approached Kharg Island — the first vessel interdicted since Washington reimposed its naval blockade — and carried out a 90-minute wave of strikes on Greater Tunb Island and military sites at Bandar Abbas. More than a thousand people rallied in Kyiv's central square — with protests also in Lviv, Odesa and Dnipro — after President Zelenskyy dismissed Defense Minister Mykhailo Fedorov, 35, credited with pioneering Ukraine's drone warfare, after just six months. Fedorov gave a press conference accusing armed-forces chief Gen. Oleksandr Syrskyi of obstructing reform, and an air force deputy commander resigned in protest as parliament confirmed a broader reshuffle. (NPR, France 24)
  • Spain and Gibraltar dismantle their border after 118 years. Border controls between Spain and the British territory were lifted as an EU-UK treaty signed in Brussels took effect, and workers began tearing down the fence. The roughly 15,000 people who cross daily can now pass with ID cards instead of stamped passports, with immigration checks shifted to Gibraltar's port and airport as the territory is effectively folded into the Schengen free-travel zone while remaining under British sovereignty. (Al Jazeera, Euronews)

⚡ Tech & AI
  • China approves Apple Intelligence, powered by Alibaba's Qwen. The Cyberspace Administration of China cleared Apple's AI service for the mainland on July 15, adding it to the official list of approved generative-AI providers. Alibaba's Qwen model will power Apple Intelligence across iOS, iPadOS, macOS and visionOS in China; the regulator did not name a launch date, and Alibaba shares rose about 4% on the news. (TechCrunch, The Next Web)
  • Apple explores buying AI chip startups to cut Nvidia reliance. Apple has held talks with bankers and semiconductor startups about acquiring AI chip companies to strengthen its AI server infrastructure and reduce dependence on Nvidia. The move follows setbacks with Apple's in-house "Baltra" server chip and reports that its Mac-based silicon struggled to run the models powering the Siri overhaul, pushing those workloads onto Nvidia hardware in Google's cloud. (9to5Mac, MacRumors)
  • MLS returns to Apple TV with no separate subscription. Major League Soccer resumed on Apple TV as the FIFA World Cup wraps, and Apple has scrapped the standalone MLS Season Pass. Every match in more than 100 countries is now included with a standard Apple TV subscription at no extra cost — a notable shift from the paid add-on model that ran since 2023. (Apple Newsroom, MacRumors)
  • Thinking Machines releases Inkling, a 975B-parameter open model. Mira Murati's Thinking Machines Lab shipped its first model: Inkling, an open-weight multimodal mixture-of-experts system with 975 billion total parameters but only about 41 billion active per task, a 1M-token context window, and a permissive Apache 2.0 license on Hugging Face. It is a direct bet against closed flagship models from OpenAI, Anthropic and Google. (TechCrunch, VentureBeat)
  • Microsoft's July Patch Tuesday sets an all-time record. Microsoft fixed roughly 570 vulnerabilities in its July update — about four times the number patched a year earlier — including three zero-days, two of them actively exploited (an Active Directory Federation Services flaw and a SharePoint Server flaw). Microsoft credited AI-assisted bug hunting for the surge and warned monthly counts will keep climbing. (Krebs on Security, TechCrunch)
