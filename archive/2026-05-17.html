No MELAS-specific or m.3243A>G papers published in the last 14 days; two items from the adjacent mitochondrial disease and gene therapy literature are included below.

  • A Scientific Reports study (Kim et al., published May 13, 2026) tested mitochondrial transplantation in patient cells carrying the m.1555A>G mtDNA mutation — a different mutation from Emi's m.3243A>G, but one causing similar oxidative phosphorylation failure. Infusing external mitochondria (PN-101, from umbilical cord mesenchymal stem cells) significantly boosted ATP and complex I activity, and — critically — shifted heteroplasmy toward wild-type mtDNA; repeated treatments sustained and strengthened this shift. The mechanism is potentially relevant to MELAS-related heteroplasmy research. ⚠️ Industry-funded: lead and corresponding authors are from Paean Biotechnology, Seoul. (doi:10.1038/s41598-026-51402-4)
  • A Cell Reports Medicine study (Pido-Lopez et al., UCL / Great Ormond Street Hospital, published May 13, 2026) engineered hematopoietic stem cells to secrete a cell-penetrating form of frataxin — the mitochondria-essential protein lost in Friedreich's ataxia — and showed that transplanting these cells delayed motor symptom onset in a mouse model, with measurable biochemical and anatomical improvement. While Friedreich's ataxia is caused by a nuclear DNA mutation, the strategy of using modified stem cells to deliver therapeutic proteins directly to mitochondria is relevant to the broader mitochondrial disease gene therapy pipeline. (doi:10.1016/j.xcrm.2026.102803)
